What are Mangroves?

You've probably heard us use the term mangroves... a lot.
But what even are mangroves?

Mangroves are a unique type of forest ecosystem that grow in coastal areas, including estuaries, lagoons, and shallow waters. These forests are composed of trees and shrubs that are uniquely adapted to thrive in salty, brackish water. Mangroves are found in tropical and subtropical regions around the world, from the Americas to Africa, Asia, and Australia.

They help

Mangrove forests provide a wide range of ecosystem services that are critical to the health of our planet. One of the most important functions of mangroves is carbon sequestration. These forests are incredibly efficient at capturing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and storing it in their biomass and sediments. Mangroves are estimated to sequester up to four times more carbon per hectare than tropical rainforests, making them a key tool in the fight against climate change.

Natural disaster prevention

In addition to their carbon sequestration capabilities, mangroves also play a critical role in protecting coastal communities from natural disasters such as storm surges and hurricanes. The dense network of roots and foliage helps to absorb the impact of waves and winds, reducing the risk of flooding and erosion. Mangroves also act as a natural water filtration system, trapping sediment and pollutants and improving water quality.

Mangroves need our help

Despite their importance, mangroves are under threat from human activities such as deforestation, aquaculture, and urbanization. The destruction of mangrove forests can have devastating consequences for both the environment and local communities. The loss of these critical ecosystems can lead to increased carbon emissions, reduced protection against natural disasters, and a decline in fisheries and other valuable resources.
